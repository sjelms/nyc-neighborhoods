## Park Slope

Park Slope is a neighborhood in South Brooklyn , New York City , within the area once known as South Brooklyn . Park Slope is roughly bounded by Prospect Park and Prospect Park West to the east, Fourth Avenue to the west, Flatbush Avenue to the north, and Prospect Expressway to the south. Generally, the neighborhood is divided into three sections from north to south: North Slope, Center Slope, and South Slope. The neighborhood takes its name from its location on the western slope of neighboring Prospect Park . Fifth Avenue and Seventh Avenue are its primary commercial streets, while its east–west side streets are lined with brownstones and apartment buildings.

---

### Key Details
- **WHAT TO EXPECT:** Park Slope is a largely mixed-use neighborhood with substantial historic building stock, concentrated ground-floor retail along Fifth and Seventh Avenues, and some mid-rise multifamily buildings fronting major corridors.


- **UNEXPECTED APPEAL:** Despite its strong residential character, Park Slope retains light industrial activity near the Gowanus Canal and offers a dense collection of landmarked structures that support destination retail, dining, cultural institutions, and community-based organizations.


- **THE MARKET:** The area is high income and not currently classified as gentrifying, with strong demand for storefronts along Fifth, Seventh, and key subway-adjacent avenues; Fourth Avenue, Fifth Avenue, and Seventh Avenue support mixed-use and low-density commercial zoning, while the western edge remains a light industrial district.

### Around the Block

Park Slope is a mature South Brooklyn neighborhood whose commercial activity is anchored by Fifth Avenue and Seventh Avenue, the primary retail streets running through the district. These corridors support a concentration of top-rated restaurants, bars, and shops, serving both local residents and visitors drawn by nearby institutions such as the Brooklyn Academy of Music, Brooklyn Museum, Brooklyn Botanic Garden, Brooklyn Conservatory of Music, and the Central Library at Grand Army Plaza. Much of the commercial space is at grade in classic brownstone and rowhouse buildings or in six-to-eight-story apartment houses with retail on the ground floor, which creates a continuous streetwall and steady pedestrian traffic. The presence of the Park Slope Food Coop on Union Street, with approximately 17,000 members, adds a unique, membership-based grocery anchor that attracts shoppers from beyond the immediate neighborhood. Smaller community institutions, including CHiPS soup kitchen and the Park Slope Volunteer Ambulance Corps, contribute to a strong nonprofit and service ecosystem that coexists with the retail mix.

From a land-use perspective, Park Slope includes manufacturing, commercial, residential, and mixed-use zoning, which creates a varied commercial landscape. Fourth, Fifth, and Seventh Avenues feature residential structures with commercial ground floors, while the westernmost portion near the Gowanus Canal is designated as a light industrial district, tying the neighborhood into Brooklyn’s traditional manufacturing and warehouse belt. The section of Seventh Avenue south of Ninth Street is largely zoned for low-density commercial use, which supports neighborhood-serving retail rather than large-format big-box development. Historic designation is a major factor shaping investment and redevelopment: the Park Slope Historic District is New York City’s largest landmark neighborhood, covering 2,575 buildings across about 40 blocks, and numerous individual landmarks—including the 14th Regiment Armory and several transit stations—anchor key corners. Transit access is a significant commercial asset, with multiple subway lines and the major Atlantic Avenue–Barclays Center hub connecting Park Slope to Manhattan and the rest of Brooklyn, reinforcing the viability of ground-floor retail and service uses. Together, these conditions make Park Slope a high-demand, transit-rich, and architecturally distinctive commercial environment with limited large-scale redevelopment but strong support for small and mid-size neighborhood businesses.
