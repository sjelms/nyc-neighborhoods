## Springfield Gardens

Springfield Gardens is a neighborhood in the southeastern area of the New York City borough of Queens, bounded to the north by St. Albans, to the east by Laurelton and Rosedale, to the south by John F. Kennedy International Airport, and to the west by Farmers Boulevard. The neighborhood is served by Queens Community Board 12. The area, particularly east of Springfield Boulevard, is sometimes also referred to as Brookville.

### Around the Block

Springfield Gardens sits in southeastern Queens with a decidedly suburban physical character, yet it is framed by significant transportation infrastructure and commercial arteries. The neighborhood is directly north of John F. Kennedy International Airport, positioning it on the edge of one of the region’s most important employment and logistics engines. This adjacency to JFK, along with cross-borough connectivity, gives the area strategic relevance for airport-related services and businesses that rely on quick access to air travel and freight corridors. Several major boulevards define the commercial grid: Farmers, Merrick, Springfield, Rockaway, and Guy R. Brewer Boulevards all serve as primary streets, carrying both local and crosstown traffic and supporting strips of small-scale retail, services, and transit-dependent commerce.

Historically, the area evolved from 17th-century farmland into a more developed community after Long Island Rail Road service expanded in the 1920s via the former Springfield Gardens station. Though that station closed in 1979, the neighborhood remains well-linked through nearby Rosedale and Laurelton LIRR stations on the Far Rockaway and Long Beach branches, which support commuter access to the broader metropolitan economy. Bus infrastructure is extensive and commercially significant: routes such as the Q3, Q6, Q85, Q86, Q87, Q89, Q111, Q113, Q114, Q115, and Q77 connect Springfield Gardens to Jamaica’s major bus terminals, Green Acres Mall, JFK Airport, and the Jamaica Center–Parsons/Archer subway hub serving the E, J, and Z trains. This bus and rail network channels customers and workers into the area’s corridors while offering outbound connectivity for residents employed elsewhere in the city. Large open spaces such as the 23.54-acre Springfield Park and 90-acre Brookville Park add recreational value and buffer some of the transportation intensity, while a registered historic district underscores the neighborhood’s long-established development pattern, which commercial uses must navigate and adapt to over time.
