## North Corona

Corona is a neighborhood in the borough of Queens in New York City . It borders Flushing and Flushing Meadows–Corona Park to the east, Jackson Heights and Elmhurst to the west, Forest Hills and Rego Park to the south, and East Elmhurst to the north. Corona's main thoroughfares include Corona Avenue, Roosevelt Avenue , Northern Boulevard , Junction Boulevard , and 108th Street.

---

### Key Details
- **WHAT TO EXPECT:** North Corona is a dense, predominantly Hispanic section of Corona north of Roosevelt Avenue, characterized by active commercial strips along Northern Boulevard, Junction Boulevard, 37th Avenue, and 108th Street that serve a largely immigrant customer base.


- **UNEXPECTED APPEAL:** The area combines historic cultural institutions such as the Louis Armstrong House and Our Lady of Sorrows Church with newer community infrastructure like the redeveloped Corona Plaza, which functions as a programmed public space supporting nearby immigrant-owned businesses.


- **THE MARKET:** With a population density above 80,000 people per square mile, North Corona offers strong foot traffic for small retailers and service businesses, a high concentration of bodegas relative to supermarkets, and generally modest area-wide household incomes, suggesting price-sensitive yet steady local demand.

### Around the Block

North Corona, defined as the part of Corona north of Roosevelt Avenue, forms a high-density commercial and residential cluster within Queens Community District 3. The neighborhood’s main arteries include Northern Boulevard, Junction Boulevard, 108th Street, and 37th Avenue, which host a wide mix of immigrant-owned storefronts, bodegas, small services, and religious institutions. These corridors are reinforced by heavy pedestrian flows from the IRT Flushing Line stations at Junction Boulevard, 103rd Street–Corona Plaza, 111th Street, and Mets–Willets Point, ensuring consistent street-level activity throughout the day. Corona Plaza itself, located at Roosevelt Avenue and National Street just south of the North Corona boundary, was transformed from an underutilized truck route and informal trading lot into a permanent pedestrian plaza. That change redirected truck activity, formalized public gathering space, and created a high-visibility platform for local vendors and cultural programming.

The redevelopment of Corona Plaza was driven by the Queens Museum and the Queens Economic Development Corporation, whose partnership explicitly aimed to strengthen neighborhood arts programming and support local businesses. Their efforts brought chairs, tables, performance space, new lighting, wayfinding, bike racks for commuters, and other amenities, making the plaza a focal node that links transit riders directly to surrounding immigrant-owned establishments. Funding and ongoing maintenance by the New York City Department of Transportation further institutionalize it as stable commercial-supporting infrastructure. North Corona’s broader environment is shaped by community-serving assets such as multiple churches, including Our Lady of Sorrows, and the nearby Langston Hughes Community Library and Cultural Center, which draw visitors for cultural and educational programming. Historically, the area has attracted notable figures in music, art, and civil rights, adding a cultural brand layer that benefits tourism-oriented businesses like the Louis Armstrong House. Coupled with strong subway and bus access and a population density over 80,000 per square mile, these elements make North Corona an attractive setting for neighborhood-scale retail, food service, and community-facing enterprises.
