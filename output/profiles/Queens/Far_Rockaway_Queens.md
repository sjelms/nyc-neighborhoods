## Far Rockaway

Far Rockaway is a neighborhood on the eastern part of the Rockaway peninsula in the New York City borough of Queens. It is the easternmost section of the Rockaways. The neighborhood extends from Beach 32nd Street east to the Nassau County line. Its southern boundary is the Atlantic Ocean ; it is one of the neighborhoods along Rockaway Beach.

### Around the Block

Far Rockaway sits on the eastern end of the Rockaway Peninsula, where the Atlantic Ocean shoreline once powered a thriving resort economy. Its early commercial story was driven by the Rockaway Association’s hotel development on former Cornell family land and by the construction of the Rockaway Turnpike and the Long Island Rail Road’s Rockaway Beach Branch to Far Rockaway station. Rail access stimulated land values and helped businesses expand, turning Far Rockaway into a relatively large village by the late 19th century. The Marine Pavilion and associated hospitality activity attracted high-profile visitors, anchoring a local service economy. Although that resort heyday declined after mid‑century, the historic bungalow district and notable institutional properties such as the Russell Sage Memorial Church, Trinity Chapel, and the Far Rockaway Post Office remain as legacy assets with adaptive reuse potential.

Over time, shifts in transportation, including the abandonment of the Rockaway Beach Branch segment in Queens and the rise of private car travel, eroded the neighborhood’s competitive position as a destination. In response, significant public housing replaced older building stock, reshaping both land use and local retail demand. More recently, city planning actions have again altered the commercial trajectory: in 2017 the City Council approved a rezoning of 23 central blocks to accommodate thousands of new residences along with community facilities and retail space, and subsequent approvals moved hundreds of new affordable units forward. The commercial spine is reinforced by robust transit infrastructure—the IND Rockaway Line A train terminal at Mott Avenue, the LIRR Far Rockaway terminal, and multiple MTA and Nassau Inter‑County Express bus routes—that facilitates commuter flows and supports neighborhood-serving businesses. Public and community facilities, including the FDNY firehouse and the $39 million, architect‑designed Far Rockaway branch of the Queens Public Library on Central Avenue, function as civic anchors and generate steady foot traffic. Together, these elements position Far Rockaway as a transit-served, lower-density oceanfront neighborhood where future commercial growth is closely tied to housing production, institutional investment, and the continued evolution of its historic built fabric.
