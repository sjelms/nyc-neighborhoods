## East Flatbush

East Flatbush is a residential neighborhood in the New York City borough of Brooklyn . East Flatbush is bounded by Crown Heights and Empire Boulevard to the north; Brownsville and East 98th Street to the east; Flatlands , Canarsie and the Long Island Rail Road 's Bay Ridge Branch to the south; and the neighborhood of Flatbush and New York Avenue to the west. East Flatbush is a predominantly black neighborhood of African American and Afro-Caribbeans and has a population of 135,619 as of the 2010 United States census .

---

### Key Details
- **WHAT TO EXPECT:** East Flatbush is a largely residential Brooklyn neighborhood with limited direct subway access but extensive bus coverage, anchored by several major hospital campuses and working- to middle-class retail along key avenues.


- **UNEXPECTED APPEAL:** The area functions as a major healthcare hub with Kings County Hospital, SUNY Downstate Medical Center, and Kingsbrook Jewish Medical Center, and includes branded cultural corridors such as Little Caribbean and Little Haiti that support Caribbean-focused businesses and services.


- **THE MARKET:** Covering about 2.9 square miles with a dense population of over 135,000, East Flatbush consists largely of working- and middle-class housing stock, including one of the city’s largest privately held working-class apartment complexes, with relatively high rent burden yet categorized as high-income and not gentrifying compared to the rest of New York City.

### Around the Block

East Flatbush presents as a dense, working- and middle-class Brooklyn neighborhood whose commercial activity is closely tied to community services and healthcare. The presence of three major hospitals—Kings County Hospital, SUNY Downstate Medical Center, and Kingsbrook Jewish Medical Center—makes healthcare one of the dominant institutional and employment anchors. These facilities generate steady daily traffic and demand for ancillary medical offices, pharmacies, food service, and convenience retail. The neighborhood’s population profile, with many middle-aged adults and youth and a high share of foreign-born Caribbean residents, underpins a customer base for small businesses tailored to West Indian and broader immigrant communities.

Culturally branded areas such as Little Caribbean around Nostrand and Church Avenues and the Little Haiti designation at the south tip of the neighborhood reinforce a specialized retail and services environment. These corridors support food, grocery, and service establishments that cater to Caribbean diasporas, and their recognition by the city (including the Newkirk Avenue–Little Haiti subway station naming) suggests durable place-based identities that can attract both local and regional customers. Longstanding residential complexes like Flatbush Gardens (formerly Vanderveer Estates), one of the largest privately held working-class housing complexes in New York City, concentrate thousands of residents within walking distance of bus routes and neighborhood-serving retail. Historic land uses, including the former Flatbush Water Works site and proximity to Paerdegat Woods and Paerdegat Basin area housing developed in the 1940s, indicate a legacy of infrastructure and postwar development that has transitioned into stable, high-density housing.

From a mobility standpoint, the neighborhood relies heavily on surface transit. Multiple MTA bus routes, including the B44/B44 SBS, B46/B46 SBS, B6, B8, B12, B35, B43, B7, B103, and BM2, stitch together commercial strips and institutional destinations, compensating for limited subway penetration into the interior blocks. Subway access is concentrated near the western border via the IRT Nostrand Avenue Line (2 and 5 trains) at Newkirk Avenue–Little Haiti, Beverly Road, and Church Avenue, and along the eastern border at Sutter Avenue–Rutland Road–East 98th Street on the IRT New Lots Line (2, 3, 4, 5 trains). This geometry shapes commercial patterns: transit-adjacent junctions and hospital perimeters are natural nodes for small-format retail, food, and personal services. Despite a history of crime and a past drug epidemic centered around key intersections such as Foster Avenue and Nostrand Avenue, reported crime has fallen sharply since the 1990s, which improves the operating environment for neighborhood businesses while the area remains predominantly local-serving rather than a regional destination district.
