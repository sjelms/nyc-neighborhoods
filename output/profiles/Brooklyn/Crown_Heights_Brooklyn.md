## Crown Heights

Crown Heights is a neighborhood in the central portion of the New York City borough of Brooklyn . Crown Heights is bounded by Washington Avenue to the west, Atlantic Avenue to the north, Ralph Avenue to the east, and Empire Boulevard to the south. It is about one mile (1.6 km) wide and two miles (3.2 km) long. Neighborhoods bordering Crown Heights include Prospect Heights to the west, Flatbush , Prospect Lefferts Gardens and East Flatbush to the south, Brownsville to the east, and Bedford–Stuyvesant to the north.

---

### Key Details
- **WHAT TO EXPECT:** Crown Heights is a centrally located Brooklyn neighborhood characterized by strong commercial corridors along Franklin, Nostrand, Kingston, and Utica Avenues, supported by dense residential blocks and good subway and bus access via the IRT Eastern Parkway and Nostrand Avenue lines.


- **UNEXPECTED APPEAL:** Eastern Parkway and Atlantic Avenue provide a mix of cultural institutions, parks, and auto-oriented commercial uses, while Kingston Avenue concentrates businesses tailored to the Hasidic community, making for a diverse and specialized retail landscape anchored by major religious and educational institutions.


- **THE MARKET:** The neighborhood has experienced rapid gentrification since the 2010s, with rising rents and active repositioning of buildings by landlords and developers, and recent rezonings on Atlantic Avenue legalizing new residential construction above historically auto-oriented commercial uses.

### Around the Block

Crown Heights sits in the center of Brooklyn and functions as a heavily trafficked commercial and institutional hub serving a dense population and multiple cultural communities. Eastern Parkway, a major tree-lined boulevard designed by Frederick Law Olmsted, cuts through the neighborhood east–west and anchors some of its most recognizable building stock. Along this spine and the adjacent avenues sit key cultural and visitor draws, including the Brooklyn Museum, Brooklyn Botanic Garden, the Brooklyn Children’s Museum, the Jewish Children’s Museum, and the Weeksville Heritage Center, all of which generate steady daytime foot traffic and tourism-related spending. The neighborhood is also home to Medgar Evers College, which brings a student and academic population into the local economy and helps support surrounding retail and service uses. Annual events like the West Indian Carnival along Eastern Parkway, which reportedly attracts millions of participants, amplify seasonal demand for food, beverage, and event-related businesses.

The commercial fabric of Crown Heights is organized around several main corridors. Franklin Avenue, Nostrand Avenue, Kingston Avenue, and Utica Avenue serve as the primary retail and services spines, with Kingston Avenue in particular concentrating businesses that serve the sizable Hasidic community centered around 770 Eastern Parkway, the worldwide headquarters of the Chabad-Lubavitch movement. These corridors run through blocks of prewar multifamily housing and row houses, providing neighborhood-scale retail, religious institutions, and community facilities. At the northern edge, Atlantic Avenue forms a distinct band of auto-oriented commercial activity, dominated by repair shops and gas stations, highlighting the area’s role in automotive services for a broader trade area. A 2025 rezoning of Atlantic Avenue between Vanderbilt and Nostrand Avenues has now legalized apartment construction above this historically low-scale, car-focused strip, signaling a shift toward more mixed-use development. Transit access is robust: multiple IRT subway lines (2, 3, 4, 5) and the Franklin Avenue Shuttle, as well as numerous bus routes, support workforce mobility and customer access to local businesses. Taken together, these elements position Crown Heights as a dense, transit-served Brooklyn submarket where cultural institutions, religious centers, and evolving commercial corridors interact to drive economic activity.
