## Marine Park

Marine Park is a neighborhood in the New York City borough of Brooklyn . The neighborhood lies between Flatlands and Mill Basin to the east, and Gerritsen Beach , Midwood , and Sheepshead Bay to the south and west. It is mostly squared off in area by Gerritsen Avenue, Flatbush Avenue , Avenue U and Kings Highway . The neighborhood's eponymous park is the largest public park in Brooklyn. Charles Downing Lay won a silver medal in town planning at the 1936 Olympics for the planning of Marine Park.

---

### Key Details
- **WHAT TO EXPECT:** Marine Park is a low-rise Brooklyn neighborhood organized around major surface corridors like Gerritsen Avenue, Flatbush Avenue, Avenue U, and Kings Highway, with commercial activity oriented along these arterials and extensive recreational open space tied to its eponymous park.


- **UNEXPECTED APPEAL:** Its proximity to Jamaica Bay, large salt marsh parkland, and the restored natural areas around Gerritsen Creek provide unusual open-space and environmental amenities for a largely built-out South Brooklyn district.


- **THE MARKET:** Marine Park shares a neighborhood tabulation area with Mill Basin, Bergen Beach, and Flatlands, with a combined population of 45,231 and a moderate density of 27.2 inhabitants per acre, supporting neighborhood-scale retail, services, and bus-fed commercial uses along primary corridors rather than rail-centric, high-density development.

### Around the Block

Marine Park’s commercial footprint is closely tied to its gridded street network and the large open space that gives the neighborhood its name. The area is mostly squared off by Gerritsen Avenue, Flatbush Avenue, Avenue U, and Kings Highway, which function as primary traffic corridors and natural locations for neighborhood-serving retail and services. Gerritsen Avenue is explicitly described as a major traffic corridor, suggesting steady vehicular flows that support convenience retail, food service, and auto-oriented businesses. Flatbush Avenue, a major Brooklyn spine that connects inland neighborhoods to the waterfront and the Belt Parkway, further anchors commercial activity, especially near the Flatbush Avenue exit of the parkway. These streets link Marine Park to adjacent neighborhoods such as Mill Basin, Flatlands, Gerritsen Beach, Midwood, and Sheepshead Bay, expanding the potential customer and workforce catchment for local businesses.

The neighborhood’s defining asset is Marine Park itself, the largest public park in Brooklyn, with 530 acres of grassland and salt marsh surrounding the westernmost inlet of Jamaica Bay. Historically, the area saw attempts at seaport and pier development in Jamaica Bay, including a large pier on former Barren Island built to receive landfill for additional piers, and subsequent land assembly that created Marine Park and nearby Floyd Bennett Field. While much of that waterfront has transitioned to recreational and conservation use, the legacy of fill operations, landfill activity, and later cleanup underscores a history of heavy-use, quasi-industrial land activity at the edges of the neighborhood. Today, the park’s sports fields, running path, and Salt Marsh Nature Center attract steady visitors, which can benefit small-format food, recreation, and service businesses on surrounding streets. Extensive bus service compensates for the lack of a local subway station: multiple routes, including the B2, B3, B9, B31, B41, B44 and B44 SBS, B46 and B46 SBS, B47, B100, BM4, and Q35, connect Marine Park to broader Brooklyn and Manhattan job centers. This bus network, combined with the Belt Parkway access at Flatbush Avenue, supports workforce mobility and customer access, making Marine Park more viable for neighborhood retail and services despite the absence of rail infrastructure.
