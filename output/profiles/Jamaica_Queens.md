## Jamaica

Jamaica is a neighborhood in the New York City borough of Queens . Jamaica is bordered by Hollis , Queens Village , St. Albans , and Cambria Heights to the east; South Jamaica , Rochdale Village , John F. Kennedy International Airport , and Springfield Gardens to the south; Laurelton and Rosedale to the southeast; Richmond Hill , South Ozone Park , and Aqueduct Racetrack to the west and southwest; Briarwood to the northwest; and Kew Gardens Hills , Jamaica Hills , and Jamaica Estates to the north.

---

### Key Details
- **WHAT TO EXPECT:** Jamaica functions as a major civic, commercial, and transportation hub for Queens, anchored by Jamaica Center along Jamaica Avenue and dense retail corridors on Hillside, Merrick, Parsons, Guy R. Brewer, and Sutphin Boulevards.


- **UNEXPECTED APPEAL:** The neighborhood combines county-level government and federal office complexes with legacy retail streets, a growing hotel cluster driven by JFK Airport and AirTrain access, and several historic commercial and institutional buildings repurposed for arts and community uses.


- **THE MARKET:** After decades of disinvestment and small discount and service retailers, rezoning around Jamaica Station, BID-led land assembly, and improving safety have attracted national chains, mixed-use projects, hotels tied to the JFK AirTrain, and steadily rising real estate prices with allowances for buildings up to 28 stories near the main transit hub.

### Around the Block

Jamaica is the administrative and transportation heart of Queens, and that role shapes nearly every aspect of its commercial landscape. The neighborhood hosts a concentration of government assets, including Queens Civil Court, the civil branch of the Queens County Supreme Court, the Queens County Family Court, and the large Joseph P. Addabbo Federal Building. That 12‑story, 932,000‑square‑foot masonry and steel complex was purpose-built for the Social Security Administration and later funded to consolidate additional federal tenants, anchoring thousands of daily workers in the area. Nearby, the U.S. Food and Drug Administration’s Northeast Regional Laboratory and New York District Office add another layer of stable institutional employment and activity.

Retail and private-sector commerce are organized around Jamaica Center and its radiating avenues. Jamaica Avenue is the busiest thoroughfare, lined with discount stores, banks, and civic institutions such as the main Queens Public Library and King Manor Museum. Hillside Avenue and Sutphin Boulevard are similarly dense with storefronts and six-lane traffic in sections, reflecting Jamaica’s role as a shopping destination for South Asian, Caribbean, and African-American communities. Decades of economic decline in the mid-20th century, when large retailers and theaters departed for the suburbs and the crack epidemic took hold, left prime spaces occupied by lower-margin tenants such as hair salons and 99-cent stores. This pattern began to shift as crime dropped and the Greater Jamaica Development Corporation and local BIDs assembled sites, provided loans, and attracted national chains and mixed-use projects like One Jamaica Center.

Transit is central to Jamaica’s commercial resurgence and logistics function. Jamaica Station is the principal Long Island Rail Road transfer point, where nearly all LIRR branches intersect and connect via the AirTrain JFK to John F. Kennedy International Airport, one of the world’s busiest airports. The expansion of Jamaica Station and the AirTrain link in the 2000s elevated the area’s value for hotels and travel-related businesses, leading to a cluster of accommodations serving airport passengers. The neighborhood’s subway infrastructure is robust, with the IND Queens Boulevard Line terminating at 179th Street and the Archer Avenue lines bringing E, J, and Z trains directly into the core. A dense bus network radiates from the 165th Street Bus Terminal and Jamaica Center, tying Jamaica to eastern Queens, the Rockaways, Nassau County, Midtown Manhattan, and the Bronx. Major arterials such as the Van Wyck Expressway, Grand Central Parkway, Rockaway Boulevard, and Union Turnpike, together with proximity to JFK, position Jamaica as a key node for regional mobility, commuting, and goods movement, supporting its evolution into a high-intensity mixed commercial district.
